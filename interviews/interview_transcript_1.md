# User Interview #1 - Recent Graduate Living Alone

**Date:** February 2026  
**Duration:** 18 minutes  
**Participant Profile:** 23 years old, recently moved into first apartment, working full-time

---

**Interviewer:** Thanks for taking the time to chat with me today. I'm curious about your experience with cooking since you've been living on your own. Can you tell me about the last time you needed to figure out what to make for dinner?

**Participant:** Oh man, that was literally yesterday. I got home from work around 7, opened my fridge, and just stared at it for like five minutes. I had some chicken that was going to expire soon, some random vegetables, and I was just like... what do I even do with this?

**Interviewer:** What did you end up doing?

**Participant:** I Googled "easy chicken recipes" and scrolled through like twenty different websites with those super long blog posts before the actual recipe. It was so annoying. I eventually just threw everything in a pan and hoped for the best. It was... edible.

**Interviewer:** How often does that happen - the not knowing what to cook situation?

**Participant:** Probably 4 or 5 times a week? I meal prep on Sundays sometimes, but by Wednesday I'm back to the same problem. I end up ordering DoorDash more than I'd like to admit.

**Interviewer:** When you do successfully cook something, what does that process look like?

**Participant:** If I'm being honest, it's usually because my mom texted me a recipe or my roommate in college showed me how to make it. Like, I know how to make pasta with marinara, scrambled eggs, and like... three other things. That's basically my rotation.

**Interviewer:** Tell me more about when your mom sends you recipes. What makes those work for you?

**Participant:** She knows what I can actually handle, you know? She's not sending me stuff that requires like fifteen ingredients or special equipment. And she usually asks what I have in my fridge first. Last month she sent me this simple stir-fry thing that used exactly what I had - chicken, bell peppers, soy sauce. Took like 20 minutes and actually tasted good.

**Interviewer:** How do you currently keep track of what ingredients you have?

**Participant:** I... don't really? I mean, I know the basics - if I have eggs, milk, bread. But like vegetables and other stuff, I usually forget until I'm already at the grocery store and I'm like "wait, do I have onions at home?" Then I buy them anyway just in case and end up with three onions.

**Interviewer:** What happened the last time you tried to learn a new recipe?

**Participant:** Oh, I tried to make this Thai curry thing I saw on Instagram like two months ago. Bought all these ingredients - lemongrass, fish sauce, coconut milk, all this stuff. Made it once, it was okay, and now all those ingredients are just sitting in my pantry because I don't know what else to use them for. Total waste of money.

**Interviewer:** That sounds frustrating. How much did that end up costing you?

**Participant:** Probably like $30-40 for ingredients I used once. And I felt bad because I was trying to be better about cooking at home to save money, but then I wasted a bunch anyway.

**Interviewer:** When you think about cooking at home versus ordering food, what factors into that decision?

**Participant:** Honestly, time and stress. If I can make something in 30 minutes or less and I don't have to think too hard about it, I'll cook. But if I have to look up recipes, go to the store for missing ingredients, follow complicated steps... I'm just ordering Chipotle.

**Interviewer:** You mentioned earlier that you know how to make three things. How did you learn those?

**Participant:** Repetition, I guess? I made scrambled eggs like every day for a month when I first moved out because it was the only thing I couldn't mess up. Now I don't even think about it. I wish I could do that with more meals but I don't know where to start.

**Interviewer:** What would have to be different for you to try learning new recipes more often?

**Participant:** I think if someone could just tell me "hey, based on what you already have, you could make X, Y, or Z" that would be huge. And if they started simple and got more complex as I got better. Right now I'll look at a recipe and it either seems too basic and boring, or way too complicated and intimidating. There's no middle ground.

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time when you felt proud of something you cooked.

**Participant:** Actually, yeah! A few weeks ago I made this sheet pan dinner - chicken, potatoes, and broccoli all roasted together. My friend came over and was like "wow, you made this?" It felt really good. But I haven't made it since because I can't remember exactly what I did, and I'm worried I'll mess it up if I try again.

**Interviewer:** What stopped you from making it again?

**Participant:** I didn't write anything down, and I kind of eyeballed everything. I'm not confident I could recreate it. Plus I don't remember what temperature or how long I cooked it for.

---

**Key Insights:**
- Participant struggles with decision paralysis when choosing what to cook
- Currently relies on memory of ~3 recipes, leading to repetitive meals
- Wastes money on single-use specialty ingredients
- Values simplicity and time efficiency (30 min or less)
- Doesn't track pantry inventory, leading to duplicate purchases
- Wants progressive difficulty - something between "too basic" and "too complex"
- Successful cooking experiences come from guided recommendations (mom's texts)
- Lack of recipe documentation prevents repeating successes
