# User Interview #3 - Aspiring Home Chef

**Date:** February 2026  
**Duration:** 25 minutes  
**Participant Profile:** 28 years old, works in marketing, lives with partner

---

**Interviewer:** Thanks for chatting with me today. I understand you're interested in getting better at cooking. Can you tell me about the last time you tried to learn a new recipe?

**Participant:** Yeah, actually just this past weekend. I've been following this food blogger who does Italian cuisine, and I tried to make homemade gnocchi from scratch. It was... ambitious.

**Interviewer:** How did it go?

**Participant:** Honestly? It was a disaster. The dough was too sticky, the gnocchi fell apart when I boiled them, and after two hours of work we ended up ordering pizza. I was so frustrated because I followed the recipe exactly, but something went wrong and I don't even know what.

**Interviewer:** That sounds really frustrating. How much time and money did that cost you?

**Participant:** Two hours of my Saturday, plus probably $25 in ingredients that we basically threw away. The worst part is I really wanted to impress my partner because they love Italian food. Instead I just felt incompetent.

**Interviewer:** You mentioned following a food blogger. How do you usually find recipes to try?

**Participant:** Oh, I'm all over the place. Instagram, Pinterest, YouTube, cookbooks I've bought. I have like six cookbooks on my shelf that I barely use. I'll get inspired by something I see online, save it, and then never actually make it.

**Interviewer:** Why don't you end up making them?

**Participant:** Usually because when I go to actually cook, I realize I'm missing like five ingredients. Then I think "okay I'll make it next week" and I never do. Or the recipe looks amazing but then I read it and realize it's a three-hour project and I'm like... not tonight.

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time when you successfully made something new and it worked out.

**Participant:** A few months ago I made this Thai basil chicken stir-fry. It was one of those 30-minute meals and I actually had most of the ingredients. It turned out great - my partner asked me to make it again. I felt like I actually knew what I was doing for once.

**Interviewer:** What made that different from the gnocchi experience?

**Participant:** I think the complexity level was more appropriate for my skill level. It was like, six ingredients, pretty straightforward steps, hard to mess up. The gnocchi was definitely outside my abilities but I didn't realize that until I was already in the middle of it.

**Interviewer:** How do you currently judge whether a recipe is at your skill level?

**Participant:** I really don't have a good system for that. I'll look at the ingredient list and if it's not too crazy I'll try it, but I don't really know what I'm getting into until I start. Sometimes something that seems simple has this one technique I've never done before and I'm suddenly watching YouTube videos trying to figure it out.

**Interviewer:** Walk me through what happens when you decide to cook dinner on a typical weeknight.

**Participant:** I get home around 6:30, I'm tired, I open the fridge and think "what can I make?" I usually end up making one of my five go-to meals because I know I won't mess them up. But then I feel bored because I'm eating the same things over and over.

**Interviewer:** What are those five go-to meals?

**Participant:** Spaghetti carbonara, chicken stir-fry, salmon with roasted vegetables, tacos, and this lemon pasta thing. They're all fine, but I've been making them for years. I want to expand my repertoire but it feels risky to try something new on a weeknight.

**Interviewer:** Why does it feel risky?

**Participant:** Because if it goes badly, we're eating at 9pm and we're both hangry and it just kills the whole vibe. Or we end up ordering delivery which defeats the purpose of trying to cook more. I'd rather stick with what I know works.

**Interviewer:** Tell me about your ingredient shopping process. How do you decide what to buy?

**Participant:** I used to meal plan on Sundays - like sit down, pick five recipes for the week, make a big shopping list, buy everything. But it never worked out because by Wednesday our plans would change or I'd be too tired to make the planned meal. So now I just keep a stocked pantry and fridge with staples and kind of freestyle it.

**Interviewer:** How does the freestyle approach work for you?

**Participant:** Better than the rigid meal planning, honestly. But I still end up with random ingredients that I don't know what to do with. Like right now I have half a head of cabbage, some chickpeas, and random herbs that I bought for specific recipes. I'm sure I could make something with them but I don't know what.

**Interviewer:** What do you do in that situation?

**Participant:** Usually I'll Google "recipes with cabbage and chickpeas" and spend 20 minutes scrolling through recipe blogs trying to find something that looks good AND that I have all the other ingredients for. By the time I find something I'm annoyed and not excited to cook anymore.

**Interviewer:** You mentioned wanting to expand your skills. What does success look like for you?

**Participant:** I want to be the person who can throw together a nice dinner without following a recipe step-by-step. Like, understanding flavor profiles and techniques well enough that I can improvise. Right now I'm completely dependent on recipes and if something goes off-script I panic.

**Interviewer:** Can you think of a time when you had to improvise and it actually worked?

**Participant:** Yeah, one time I was making a curry and realized I was out of coconut milk. I used heavy cream instead and it actually turned out really good. My partner was like "you should make it this way from now on." That felt amazing because I made an actual cooking decision that worked.

**Interviewer:** What gave you the confidence to try that substitution?

**Participant:** I didn't really have a choice - we were already cooking and I wasn't going to the store. But also I had made that specific curry like ten times before, so I felt comfortable enough with the recipe to experiment a little.

**Interviewer:** So familiarity with the recipe gave you confidence to experiment?

**Participant:** Yeah exactly. I wish I had that familiarity with more recipes. But it takes so long to get there - you have to make something multiple times before you really understand it. I don't have the patience or time for that with every recipe.

**Interviewer:** Last question - if you could change one thing about your cooking experience right now, what would it be?

**Participant:** I wish there was a way to know what I can make with what I already have, and have it be something new and interesting that's also at my skill level. I feel like I'm either making the same old stuff or attempting something way too hard. There's no middle path that helps me gradually get better while still being practical for weeknight cooking.

---

**Key Insights:**
- Participant has base cooking skills but wants to progress beyond beginner level
- Struggles to accurately assess recipe difficulty before attempting
- Experiences "decision fatigue" when choosing what to cook
- Limited repertoire (5 recipes) due to fear of failure on busy weeknights
- Wastes ingredients from abandoned recipes or impulse purchases
- Desires gradual skill progression with confidence-building
- Values improvisation ability but lacks confidence to experiment
- Time constraints (6:30pm arrival home) limit willingness to try complex recipes
- Repetition builds confidence but takes significant time investment
- Wants recommendations that match current skill level and available ingredients
- Has abandoned rigid meal planning in favor of flexible approach
- Willing to invest time on weekends but needs weeknight-appropriate options
