# User Interview #2 - College Student

**Date:** February 2026  
**Duration:** 22 minutes  
**Participant Profile:** 20 years old, junior in college, lives in apartment with roommates

---

**Interviewer:** Thanks for meeting with me. I'd love to hear about how you handle meals during the school year. Can you walk me through what happened the last time you had to figure out dinner?

**Participant:** Sure, so last Thursday I got back from class around 6pm. My roommates had already eaten and I opened the fridge and we had basically nothing. Like, some eggs, half a bell pepper, random condiments. I was broke until my paycheck hit on Friday, so ordering out wasn't an option.

**Interviewer:** What did you do?

**Participant:** I made what my friends and I call "college special" - basically scrambled eggs with whatever vegetables we can find and put it over rice. We always have rice because it's cheap and lasts forever. It wasn't great but it filled me up.

**Interviewer:** How often do you find yourself in that situation - limited ingredients, limited money?

**Participant:** Like... most of the week? We usually do a big grocery run on Sundays when we all have time, but by Thursday or Friday we're scraping by. Between the four of us, we're all on different schedules so it's hard to coordinate.

**Interviewer:** Tell me about that Sunday grocery shopping trip. How do you decide what to buy?

**Participant:** It's honestly kind of chaotic. We make a shared list on our phones throughout the week, but then we get to the store and someone's like "oh we should get this" or "this is on sale." We end up buying stuff without really having a plan for what we're going to make with it. Then by Wednesday we're looking at random ingredients like "what do we do with eggplant?"

**Interviewer:** What happened the last time you had an eggplant situation - ingredients but no plan?

**Participant:** Oh god, we bought zucchini once because it was on sale, like three of them. Nobody knew how to cook it. We tried to stir fry it but it got all mushy and gross. Two of them went bad in the fridge. Felt really wasteful, especially on our budget.

**Interviewer:** You mentioned budget is a concern. How much do you typically spend on groceries?

**Participant:** We try to keep it around $40-50 per person per week. Some weeks it works, some weeks it doesn't. The problem is we waste a lot because we don't use everything we buy.

**Interviewer:** When you DO successfully make a meal, what makes that work?

**Participant:** Usually it's because someone's mom sent them a recipe, or we look up "easy college meals" on TikTok. There's this one creator who does like 5-ingredient meals and those are clutch. Simple, cheap, and we can actually follow along.

**Interviewer:** Can you think of a specific time one of those worked out well?

**Participant:** Yeah! My roommate found this video for a pasta bake thing - just pasta, marinara sauce, cheese, and frozen spinach. We had everything except the spinach, ran to the corner store for that, and made it. It fed all four of us for like $12 total. We were hyped about it.

**Interviewer:** Did you make it again after that?

**Participant:** We tried to, but we couldn't find the video again. She didn't save it and TikTok's algorithm is weird, so we just never found it. We tried to recreate it from memory but it wasn't the same.

**Interviewer:** That's interesting. Do you usually save recipes anywhere?

**Participant:** Sometimes I'll screenshot them or save them on TikTok or Instagram, but then I never can find them when I actually need them. I have like hundreds of food videos saved but no organization. It's a mess.

**Interviewer:** Let's talk about your cooking skill level. How would you describe yourself in the kitchen?

**Participant:** Beginner for sure. I can make like basic stuff - pasta, rice, eggs, grilled cheese. My roommate knows how to make actually good meals because her dad taught her, so sometimes she'll show us. But on my own? Pretty limited.

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time your roommate taught you something.

**Participant:** She showed me how to make chicken fajitas a couple months ago. We had chicken, peppers, onions, and tortillas. She walked me through cutting everything, seasoning it, cooking it in the right order. I felt like a real chef for a second. But I haven't tried making it on my own because I'm worried I'll forget a step or burn something.

**Interviewer:** What would need to be different for you to try making it yourself?

**Participant:** I guess if I had it written down somewhere with like actual measurements and clear steps? She was just eyeballing everything and saying "cook until it looks done" which is not helpful when you don't know what "done" looks like.

**Interviewer:** How do you currently decide what to eat on any given night?

**Participant:** Usually it's just "what's fastest" or "what takes the least brain power." After classes and work I'm exhausted. If something requires more than like three steps I'm probably not doing it.

**Interviewer:** What's the most complicated thing you've made recently?

**Participant:** I made tacos from scratch last week - seasoned the meat, chopped vegetables, made a little pico de gallo situation. It took me like 45 minutes and honestly by the time I sat down to eat I was so tired I barely enjoyed it. My roommates were impressed though.

**Interviewer:** Was it worth the effort?

**Participant:** Taste-wise, yes. It was way better than anything I usually make. Time-wise? I don't know if I'd do it again on a weeknight. Maybe on a weekend when I'm not stressed.

**Interviewer:** Last question - when you think about learning to cook better, what's holding you back?

**Participant:** Time, money, and honestly just not knowing where to start. There's so much information out there but nothing that's like "hey, you're a broke college student with basic skills, start here and work your way up." Everything is either too simple like "make toast" or too complicated like "make beef wellington." I just want something in between that grows with me.

---

**Key Insights:**
- Participant operates under severe budget constraints ($40-50/week)
- Shared living situation creates coordination challenges
- Wastes food due to buying ingredients without recipes planned
- Limited cooking knowledge but motivated to learn
- Relies heavily on social media (TikTok) for recipe discovery
- Difficulty re-finding saved recipes later
- Values speed and simplicity (3 steps or less on busy nights)
- Wants progressive learning system ("grows with me")
- Lacks confidence to recreate dishes without explicit instructions
- Time poverty during weeknights limits cooking ambition
